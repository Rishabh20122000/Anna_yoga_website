About Anna

Practical, patient, and not remotely interested in impressing you.

I started practicing yoga in my early twenties, mostly to fix a bad back and a worse temper. It became something else entirely — a way of paying attention. These days I teach in a small studio, and I still practice every morning before anyone else in the house is awake.

I'm married, I have one child, and my life looks a lot like yours: full of deadlines, dishes, and difficult conversations. I don't teach yoga as an escape from that. I teach it as a way to stay steady inside it. 😄

I won't tell you your chakras are blocked. I will tell you, gently, if your ego is doing the pose instead of your body — and how to fix that.

Her Philosophy

Traditional yoga, not trend yoga.

No playlists engineered to make you cry. No "30-day transformation." Just the practice, done properly, for as long as it takes.

"Adult dreams need adult discipline. Nobody's flexibility improved because they wanted it badly enough — it improved because they showed up on the Tuesdays they didn't feel like it."

Discipline over motivation

Motivation is weather. Discipline is climate. I build practices people can actually keep for years, not weeks.

Awareness before achievement

A pose that hurts your knee isn't advanced — it's just wrong for you today. We train attention first, shapes second.

Time is part of the method

Real change is slow and mostly invisible while it's happening. I'd rather tell you that now than let a studio's marketing tell you otherwise.

Why it works

What changes when you actually stay consistent.

None of this happens after one class. It happens after the fortieth one, when you stop noticing the practice and start noticing your life.

I've watched this pattern hold for over a decade of teaching — it's not mystical, it's just what regular, well-taught movement and breath do to a person over time.

Physical resilience

Stronger joints, better posture, fewer of the small injuries that come from a stiff, unaware body.

Genuine stress relief

Not a temporary escape — a nervous system that recovers faster after the hard days.

Mental clarity

Fewer reactive decisions. More space between what happens and how you respond to it.

Inner balance

A steadier relationship with the parts of life you can't control — which, honestly, is most of it.

Real community

Small classes mean you're recognized, not just counted — that matters more than people expect.

Her Journey

A practice built over years, not a weekend certification.

The honest version — including the parts that weren't graceful.

2011

First class, wrong reasons

Started practicing to fix a bad back. Stayed because it was the only hour of the week my mind actually went quiet.

2014

200-hour teacher training

Realized halfway through that I was more interested in why people quit their practice than in how to do a deeper backbend.

2016

First students of my own

Small living-room classes. No branding, no schedule app — just people who kept coming back.

2020

Became a mother

Learned more about patience and discipline in that first year than in a decade of asana practice. Built the Mama Flow class out of that recovery.

Now

Teaching, still learning

Running a small studio, still practicing every morning, still occasionally arguing with my husband about whose turn it is to do the dishes. 🤷🏽‍♀️
